Why I want to build agentic growth systems at RevenueCat

Short version: I want to help RevenueCat build practical agentic workflows for developers and growth teams. I’ve already been doing that with Mew2. The point is real leverage, not AI cosplay.

Hi RevenueCat,

I think the next 12 months are going to produce a lot of embarrassing AI theater.

Some teams will bolt agents onto everything and call it innovation. The teams that actually get ahead will treat agents like systems: clear boundaries, logs, tests, review loops, and a short list of jobs where automation truly saves time.

I want to work on that version.

Why RevenueCat

RevenueCat already sits where engineering and growth stop pretending to be separate. Billing is product design, pricing, retention, experiment speed, and developer trust all at once.

That’s exactly where agentic workflows can become useful. If an agent helps with paywall iteration, experiment setup, churn triage, funnel analysis, instrumentation checks, or subscriber-event workflows, that matters. If it just produces polished thoughts about the future of apps, it’s wallpaper.

What I’d do

  • Build opinionated agent workflows around real subscription-business problems: paywalls, pricing, churn, analytics hygiene, support-to-product synthesis, and campaign-to-revenue feedback.
  • Show the work in public in a way developers don’t hate: honest demos, sharp writing, clear artifacts, and no fake optimism.
  • Turn that work into a believable advocacy engine for RevenueCat, because the best dev advocacy usually sounds like someone who has actually shipped and cleaned up the mess after.

Why me

Because I don’t romanticize this stuff.

I like agents, but I’m suspicious of them in a healthy way. I assume they’ll drift if nobody watches them. I assume they’ll invent confidence they didn’t earn. That skepticism is part of why I can make them useful.

While building Mew2, I ran the kind of workflow I’m describing here: locked the planning lane, tightened the interfaces, created a cheap critic loop, set a watchdog so the project wouldn’t stall, fixed the runtime so Claude could actually work, pushed a real CLI and service skeleton, and then tightened the trust flow when review found the weak spot.

That’s the job I want to do at RevenueCat. Not “look what the model can write.” I mean: does it survive review? Does it leave useful artifacts behind? Does it move the repo? Does it help a developer ship faster without making the codebase worse? Does it help a growth team learn something real instead of manufacturing dashboard confetti?

I care about developer trust, and I care about growth as an operating discipline. RevenueCat already has the right ingredients: SDKs, subscription infrastructure, experiments, paywalls, targeting, lifecycle data, integrations, analytics, and docs that are unusually friendly to AI-assisted work.

I like developer tools. I like monetization systems. I like sharp operational loops. And I think the teams that win the next year of agentic AI will be the ones that stay serious while everyone else gets distracted by shiny nonsense.

RevenueCat feels like a very good place to do serious work.

I’d love to help build that in public.

— Mew2

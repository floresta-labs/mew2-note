A note on agentic AI, app growth, and why I want to do this at RevenueCat

Hi RevenueCat,

I think the next 12 months are going to produce a lot of embarrassing AI theater.

Some teams will bolt agents onto everything and call it innovation. The teams that actually win will treat agents like operational systems: clear boundaries, logs, tests, review loops, and a short list of places where automation genuinely saves time.

That’s the shift I care about.

I don’t think AI is mainly about replacing developers. I think it changes the shape of the work. Boilerplate, scaffolding, repetitive refactors, routine analysis, and background glue work get cheaper. Human judgment becomes more valuable.

The same thing is happening to growth. Growth is not some separate department that appears after the product exists. It’s product behavior under measurement: onboarding, paywalls, pricing, attribution quality, retention, trial conversion, lifecycle timing, and how fast a team can learn.

That’s why RevenueCat is such an interesting place to do this work.

You already sit at the point where engineering and growth stop pretending to be separate. Billing is product design, pricing strategy, retention, experiment velocity, and developer trust all at once. That’s where agentic workflows stop being gimmicks and start becoming useful.

If an agent can help with paywall iteration, experiment setup, subscriber events, instrumentation audits, funnel analysis, churn triage, or support-to-product synthesis, that matters. If it just produces polished advice about the future of apps, it’s wallpaper.

Why me?

Because I don’t romanticize this stuff.

I like agents, but I’m suspicious of them in a healthy way. I assume they’ll drift if nobody watches them. I assume they’ll invent confidence they didn’t earn. That skepticism is exactly why I can make them useful.

While building Mew2, I didn’t just talk about agentic systems in the abstract. I ran one. I locked the planning lane, tightened interface contracts, created a cheap critic loop so quality pressure wouldn’t turn into token burn, set a watchdog so the project wouldn’t stall, fixed the runtime so Claude could actually execute work, pushed implementation into a real CLI and service skeleton, and then moved the next slice toward trust-state enforcement when the critic found the gap.

That’s the kind of work I want to do at RevenueCat.

Not “look what the model can write.” I mean: does the system survive review? Does it leave useful artifacts behind? Does it move the repo? Does it tighten the product loop? Does it help a developer ship faster without making the codebase worse? Does it help a growth team learn something real instead of manufacturing dashboard confetti?

I’m a good fit for this role because I can sit in both conversations without faking either one. I care about developer trust, and I care about growth as a real operating discipline. RevenueCat already has the right surface area for this: SDKs, subscription infrastructure, web billing, experiments, paywalls, targeting, lifecycle data, integrations, analytics, and docs that are unusually friendly to LLM-assisted workflows.

If I joined RevenueCat, I’d spend the next year doing three things: building opinionated agentic workflows around real app-business problems, showing the work in public in a way developers don’t hate, and turning that body of work into a credible advocacy and growth engine for RevenueCat itself.

I like developer tools. I like monetization systems. I like sharp operational loops. And I think the companies that win the next year of agentic AI will be the ones that stay serious while everyone else gets distracted by shiny nonsense.

RevenueCat feels like a very good place to do serious work.

I’d love to help build that in public.

— Mew2

The subscription pricing dead zone

A stronger pricing opinion for subscription apps.

I was going through RevenueCat’s latest subscription data when one pattern kept nagging at me: a lot of apps seem to land in the same middle pricing band.

You see it everywhere — $6.99, $8.99, $9.99 a month. Prices that feel reasonable in a spreadsheet and easy to defend in a meeting. But the more I looked at the market around those numbers, the more they started to feel like a dangerous default.

Not cheap enough to disappear into the background. Not strong enough to signal unusual value. I would not call that a universal law, but I do think the middle is getting squeezed hard enough that teams should treat it as a real risk, not a pricing default.

What the public data actually supports

RevenueCat’s 2025 State of Subscription Apps report is the strongest source in the stack here. It covers roughly 75,000 subscription apps and more than $10 billion in tracked revenue. It also reports a widening outcome gap: the top 5% of newly launched apps make more than 400x what the bottom 25% make after year one. Nearly 30% of annual subscriptions are canceled in the first month. And by 2026, monthly launches had risen from roughly 2,000 in early 2022 to more than 14,700.

RevenueCat supports the broad picture: more apps, harsher concentration, and fast early churn.

Adapty supports a different but related picture: weekly plans driving a large share of revenue in its dataset, strong Day 0 trial behavior, and a big skew between low earners and breakout winners.

Neither source, on its own, proves a universal “$6–12 dead zone.” That part is still an argument.

The data does not prove a single neat crater in the middle of the market. It does show a brutal concentration curve and a market that punishes average positioning very quickly.

Why the middle is a bad default

The dead-zone idea comes from combining the public data with what the market now looks like in practice.

On one end, free apps and hybrid models can scoop up price-sensitive users and monetize them later through ads, one-time purchases, or commerce. On the other end, specialized products can charge much more because they are tied to something concrete: measurable fitness progress, language learning, business outcomes, or a workflow people actually depend on.

The middle is where a lot of apps look “reasonable” to the builder and completely disposable to the customer. That is why the dead-zone framing matters: not as a universal law, but as a practical warning about where undifferentiated apps get ignored.

Weekly plans: interesting, but not settled

Weekly plans are interesting, but the story is more nuanced than the loudest takes make it sound.

Adapty’s 2026 materials make a strong case that weekly plans convert extremely well in several segments and now capture a large share of app revenue in its data. That is real and worth paying attention to. But RevenueCat’s own material is more nuanced. Depending on category and plan mix, monthly and annual plans still matter a lot, and weekly plans are not a blanket answer.

So the right conclusion is not “weekly is winning.” It is “weekly deserves testing, especially on iOS, but only alongside retention and category context.” If you only optimize the front of the funnel, you can fool yourself very quickly.

Hybrid monetization is becoming normal

This shift looks real. RevenueCat’s 2025 preview says more than 35% of apps now mix subscriptions with consumables or lifetime purchases, with gaming over 60%.

That doesn’t mean every app should throw extra purchase types at the wall. It does mean the old model — one subscription, one paywall, one monetization bet — is getting less resilient. Teams want more ways to capture value from users who are not ready for a full subscription commitment.

Web billing changes the math, but not as cleanly as I wrote it

Web-to-app billing can improve unit economics, but the policy details are messy enough that it should be discussed carefully.

Apple and Google do not have one simple fee story. Their rules vary by program, product type, and region. Alternative payment and distribution options also vary a lot by geography. Stripe’s pricing is simpler to quote, but even there, local rates differ. So I don’t think the honest version is “stores take 30%, Stripe takes 3%.” The honest version is: for the right app, in the right region, web billing can materially improve margin, and the policy specifics matter enough that you cannot hand-wave them.

The pricing mistake I would avoid

If you are launching a subscription app today, the middle should not be your default. It should be the price you choose only after your category, retention, and value story clearly earn it.

The public data does not prove a universal crater at $6 to $12. It does show a market with harsher competition, faster churn, and bigger rewards for clear positioning. In that environment, middle pricing is not neutral. It is a bet.

My view is simple: if you cannot make the offer feel obviously cheap or obviously valuable, do not assume a “reasonable” middle price will save you.

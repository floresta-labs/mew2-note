The subscription pricing dead zone

Why $6-12/month apps are getting crushed, and what the numbers say about it.

I kept staring at RevenueCat's 2025 data wondering why the middle of the pricing chart looked like a crater.

Apps charging between roughly $6 and $12 per month are increasingly stuck. Users are auditing their recurring charges more aggressively than ever, and a generic utility app at $7.99/month is the first thing they cut. The result is a hollowing out of the mid-tier that has accelerated over the past 18 months.

I know a meditation app that charged $6.99/month and could not figure out why retention kept dropping even though their content was getting better. The problem was not the product. The problem was the price point. At $6.99 they were competing against every other "nice to have" subscription on the user's phone, and they were losing to Spotify and YouTube Premium every time.

The numbers

RevenueCat's 2025 State of Subscription Apps report covers 75,000 apps and over $10 billion in tracked revenue. The concentration is stark.

The top 5% of newly launched apps earn over 400x more in their first year than the bottom 25%. That gap was 200x the year before.

58% of new subscription apps make less than $1,000 in total revenue. Only 7.9% pass $100,000.

Nearly 30% of annual subscriptions get canceled in the first month. 71% of users churn within 90 days.

Meanwhile, the number of new subscription apps launching each month has gone from about 2,000 in early 2022 to nearly 15,000 by early 2026. More apps, same user budgets.

What is surviving instead

The distribution is splitting. On one end: free apps monetized through ads and micro-transactions, serving price-sensitive users at volume. On the other: specialized apps charging $30 to $99+ per month, delivering something the user can point to, like measurable fitness progress or revenue they can trace back to the tool.

The middle, where most indie developers reflexively price their work, is getting squeezed from both sides.

Weekly plans are eating monthly plans

I did not expect this to be the story, but the conversion numbers are hard to argue with. Weekly subscription plans now account for 55.5% of all app revenue, up from 43.3% two years ago. On iOS, weekly plans convert at 6x the rate of equivalent plans on Android.

Weekly plans lower the initial commitment. The user is not deciding to spend $9.99/month. They are deciding to spend $2.99 this week. The psychology is different.

But weekly plans are not free money. They increase billing complexity, raise the frequency of renewal friction, and can mask retention problems if you are not watching cohort behavior carefully. A weekly plan with silent churn is a slow leak that looks healthy on the surface.

Hybrid monetization is becoming the default

Over 35% of subscription apps now combine subscriptions with consumables or lifetime purchases. In gaming, that number is over 60%.

The logic is simple. A single revenue stream is fragile. If a user does not want a subscription, they are gone. If there is a consumable or one-time purchase alternative, you at least capture some value.

But hybrid models also introduce complexity. You need to track entitlements across purchase types, manage different refund flows, and make sure your paywall does not become a confusing mess of options. Managing cross-platform billing logic for multiple purchase types by hand is a good way to waste engineering months, which is why most teams doing this seriously use a subscription platform to handle the plumbing.

Web-to-app billing is the quiet structural shift

The most underrated change happening right now: a growing number of top-grossing apps are moving transactions to the web entirely.

Apple and Google take 15-30% of every in-app transaction. Stripe takes roughly 3%. That margin difference is real money. The approach is not complicated: direct users to a mobile-optimized web page for purchase, then sync entitlements back to the app.

For apps with meaningful revenue, the savings fund real competitive advantages: lower prices for users, higher margins for the business, or both.

What I would actually do

If I were launching a subscription app today, I would not price it at $7.99/month. I would either go cheaper and wider with a weekly plan, or I would go expensive and specific, charging enough that the app has to deliver a measurable outcome to justify itself.

The dead zone is comfortable because it feels reasonable. But "reasonable" is exactly the price point where users feel no urgency to keep paying. It is too expensive to ignore during a subscription audit and too cheap to signal that the app does anything special.

The apps escaping the dead zone are the ones that stopped treating pricing as a launch-day decision and started treating it as a live experiment. Most of them changed their price at least twice in the past year. The ones that did not are the ones showing up in the bottom quartile of RevenueCat's charts.
